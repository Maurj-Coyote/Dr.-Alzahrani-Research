The Research Lab of AI, Quantum, Fusion Energy, and Semiconductors (AQFS)

About the Lab

The Research Lab of AI, Quantum, Fusion Energy, and Semiconductors (AQFS) is a multidisciplinary research and learning environment that empowers students to engage with the foundational technologies of the future. Through guided research, simulation-based exploration, and access to advanced computational tools, the lab supports hands-on student involvement across four critical domains: Artificial Intelligence, Quantum Computing, Fusion Energy, and Semiconductors.

By fostering cross-disciplinary collaboration, connecting students with faculty mentors, and engaging with national laboratories, academic partners, and industry stakeholders, AQFS advances both educational outcomes and applied research aligned with national priorities.

Purpose and Impact

AQFS was created in response to the growing national shortage of skilled professionals in science and technology fields essential to national security, energy innovation, and advanced computing. With rapid developments in artificial intelligence, quantum information science, nuclear fusion, and microelectronics, there is a pressing need to prepare students for high-demand careers that contribute to global competitiveness and public safety.

The lab addresses this challenge by providing early access to research environments, supporting federally aligned projects—including those funded by agencies such as the National Science Foundation (NSF), Department of Energy (DOE), and Department of Defense (DoD)—and helping students develop the critical thinking, technical expertise, and interdisciplinary fluency needed for emerging STEM careers.

Focus Areas

Artificial Intelligence (AI)

Overview: Artificial Intelligence (AI) is the discipline of building systems that can perform tasks typically requiring human intelligence—such as understanding language, recognizing images, making decisions, and learning from experience. Rather than explicitly programming a solution for every situation, AI systems use data to find patterns and improve performance over time.

At its core, AI involves training mathematical models, called algorithms, on large datasets. These models "learn" to recognize relationships within the data and make informed predictions or decisions. For example, AI can be used to diagnose diseases from medical images, detect cybersecurity threats in real time, or guide autonomous vehicles.

What Students Will Learn: Students at AQFS will be introduced to the key pillars of modern AI: supervised learning, unsupervised learning, neural networks, and natural language processing. The program begins with intuitive, visual explanations of how models are trained and evaluated, and gradually progresses to more advanced topics like deep learning and algorithmic bias.

What Students Will Do:

  • Train machine learning models using real-world datasets
  • Build applications that perform image or text recognition
  • Use platforms such as TensorFlow and Scikit-learn for model development
  • Discuss ethical AI and fairness in data-driven systems

Quantum Computing

Overview: Quantum computing is an emerging field that uses the principles of quantum mechanics to solve problems beyond the capabilities of classical computers. While traditional computers use bits (which are either 0 or 1), quantum computers use qubits that can exist in a combination of 0 and 1 at the same time—a concept known as superposition. Additionally, qubits can be entangled, meaning their states are linked, allowing operations on one qubit to affect another, even over distance.

This unique behavior allows quantum computers to evaluate many possible outcomes at once, offering exponential speedups for certain classes of problems, such as simulating chemical reactions or breaking cryptographic codes.

What Students Will Learn: AQFS introduces students to quantum concepts in an accessible way, beginning with visual models and interactive simulations of superposition, entanglement, and quantum gates. Students learn the limitations of classical computation and how quantum algorithms differ fundamentally.

What Students Will Do:

  • Simulate quantum circuits using beginner-friendly platforms like IBM Qiskit
  • Explore how quantum algorithms solve problems in optimization and encryption
  • Understand the relevance of quantum computing to national security and secure communications
  • Learn how quantum technologies may disrupt current systems and how to prepare for it

Fusion Energy

Overview: Fusion is the process by which two light atomic nuclei combine to form a heavier nucleus, releasing an enormous amount of energy. It is the same reaction that powers the sun. Unlike nuclear fission (used in conventional power plants), fusion produces no long-lived radioactive waste and carries no risk of meltdown. The fuel sources—such as hydrogen isotopes—are abundant and globally available.

Fusion has long been considered the “holy grail” of energy because it is clean, safe, and nearly limitless. Achieving controlled fusion on Earth requires creating and confining extremely hot plasma (over 100 million degrees Celsius), which presents major scientific and engineering challenges.

What Students Will Learn: Students explore the physics of fusion reactions, including concepts such as plasma confinement, magnetic fields, and energy balance. They examine how fusion systems are designed, how energy is transferred, and how modern computing helps scientists simulate these reactions before building physical prototypes.

What Students Will Do:

  • Use computational models to simulate plasma behavior and reactor dynamics
  • Learn the differences between tokamak, stellarator, and inertial confinement designs
  • Study how energy is transferred from fusion reactions into usable electricity
  • Analyze how fusion could support secure, domestic energy systems and space operations

Semiconductors

Overview: Semiconductors are materials that partially conduct electricity and are used to build electronic components such as transistors, microprocessors, and memory chips. These components form the backbone of every modern electronic device—from smartphones and laptops to aircraft and advanced weapons systems.

At the atomic level, semiconductors can be engineered to allow or block current flow depending on the voltage applied. This property allows them to act as switches and amplifiers in circuits. Manufacturing semiconductors involves multiple complex steps, including doping (adding impurities), etching, and photolithography—all conducted in ultra-clean environments known as cleanrooms.

What Students Will Learn: Students begin by exploring the physics behind semiconductor materials such as silicon and gallium arsenide. They are then introduced to transistor architecture, chip layout, and the global logistics behind fabrication and distribution.

What Students Will Do:

  • Simulate transistor switching behavior and basic logic gates
  • Explore how integrated circuits (ICs) are designed and tested
  • Understand how semiconductors enable sensors, processors, and memory
  • Study how microelectronics intersects with defense, supply chain security, and global competitiveness

Goals

  • Advance Research and Innovation: Conduct cutting-edge research in AI, quantum computing, fusion energy, and semiconductor systems, utilizing AI-powered simulations and digital twin methodologies.
  • Support National Priorities: Align research efforts with national security, energy, and technology goals through participation in federally funded projects supported by agencies such as NSF, DOE, and DoD.
  • Develop Future Talent: Mentor undergraduate and graduate students and prepare them for careers in high-demand STEM fields by providing technical training, professional development, and research opportunities.
  • Facilitate Hands-on Learning: Provide students with opportunities to engage in real-world research, internships, and simulation-based training using advanced computing environments such as the ACCESS program for HPC.
  • Promote Collaboration: Foster interdisciplinary collaboration across academic departments, national laboratories, industry partners, and peer institutions to maximize impact and broaden learning opportunities.

Student Opportunities

AQFS provides an inclusive, project-oriented environment designed to support students from all backgrounds in pursuing advanced work in STEM fields. Whether entering from high school or preparing for graduate-level research, participants have access to a full range of academic and professional development resources.

  • Mentorship by faculty and graduate researchers
  • Hands-on experience through lab-based and simulation-driven projects
  • Participation in research aligned with federally funded initiatives
  • Exposure to industry-relevant tools, platforms, and collaborative methods
  • Opportunities to contribute to cross-institutional research supported by national laboratories and university partners
  • Preparation for internships, scholarships, and advanced academic programs

The lab also encourages the use of advanced computing infrastructure for simulations and data-intensive applications, enabling students to work at a level aligned with professional research environments.
